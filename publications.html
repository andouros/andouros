Real-world effects of direct oral anticoagulants

In the past years, I studied the use of direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) in various populations https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28984052 & https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34409636 and assessed important safety issues such as their risk of liver injury https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29519351/, esophageal disorders https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32377758/, and bleeding when used together with antiplatelets https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30691551/ & https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35036824/. Finally, I assessed their effectiveness and safety in high-risk populations such as older adults https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36150513/ and patients with cancer related thrombosis https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33836492/. Currently, I am studying their effectiveness and safety in patients with atrial fibrillation and liver disease, in a project funded by CIHR. Moreover, I am studying the safety of drug-drug interactions involving DOACs with my trainees Fabian and Jenny.

Safety of sulfonylureas

Given the use of sulfonylureas at different stages of type 2 diabetes, I assessed their safety both as first-line https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28864502/ and second-line treatments https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30021781/. I also specifically assessed the safety of glimepiride, a sulfonylurea commonly used as active comparator in randomized trials assessing the effects of newer antidiabetic drugs https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31596043/. Recently, several studies funded by CIHR and led by my trainee Jenny focused on the risk of severe hypoglycemia due to concomitant use of sulfonylureas and other medications https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35984011 & https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36525638 & https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37326010.

Safety of sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitors

In 2020, I was selected to lead an international, multicenter study conducted by the Canadian Network for Observational Drug Effect Studies (CNODES), one of the largest pharmacoepidemiologic networks in the world. The study investigated the risk of diabetic ketoacidosis with sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, an important safety concern according to several regulatory agencies. We observed an almost 3- fold increased risk of diabetic ketoacidosis associated with SGLT2 inhibitors https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32716707/. Outside of CNODES, I have also studies their risk of venous thromboembolism https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33316246/ and the safety of their interaction with statins https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36912450/.
